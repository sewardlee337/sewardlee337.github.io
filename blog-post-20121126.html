The Federal Reserve's Fuzzy Language Problem

  • Home
  • Blog
  • Blog post

November 26, 2012

Any undergraduate econ student can attest to the fact that, short of popping Valium, nothing puts you to sleep faster than trudging through Federal Reserve documents and reports.

So while we pay attention to headline-grabbing policies such as quantitative easing, we often forget that communication – what the Federal Reserve says, how it says it, and whether people believe what it says – is in and of itself a tool in the Fed’s policy toolkit. As our economy struggles with anemic growth, what we need from the Fed is clear and precise language on what it’s doing and intends to do regarding inflation.

A good communications policy is all the more important in our current economy because of its connection to the liquidity trap – a major contributing factor to our prolonged slump. Usually, when an economy faces a recession, its central bank responds by lowering real interest rates accordingly. Lower real interest rates lead to an increase in investment spending, thereby counteracting the fall in output. But the economy finds itself in a liquidity trap when its nominal interest rate nears the zero lower bound. Nominal interest rates can’t go below zero, causing real interest rates to become stuck as well. The central bank therefore can’t continue to increase output by slashing interest rates. The U.S. economy has been constrained by the zero lower bound since December 2008, and since then the Fed’s conventional tool for fighting output shortfalls has been impotent.



Source: The Fiscal Times

Here is where communication can play a role. One way to free the economy from the liquidity trap is to raise households' and firms' expectation of future inflation. If expected inflation rises, it lowers the real interest rate at which nominal rates are zero. The Federal Reserve therefore needs to convince people that future inflation will be higher than what they currently expect – higher than the Fed’s 2% long-run inflation target. The fancy term used by economists for this strategy is “forward guidance”.

So how fares the Federal Reserve’s current communications policy? Not well, but there are recent signs that suggest positive developments are on the way. At the moment, the Federal Open Market Committee has stuck to wishy-washy statements that don’t give us any reason to expect higher future inflation. For example, an FOMC statement from September said:

If the outlook for the labor market does not improve substantially, the Committee will continue its purchases of agency mortgage-backed securities, undertake additional asset purchases, and employ its other policy tools as appropriate until such improvement is achieved in a context of price stability.

What does this tell us? Nothing substantive. It’s the usual yawn-inducing Fedspeak that gives us no reason to expect future inflation to be higher than 2%.

The positive signs are that some members of the Fed are coming to see that clear, precise, and assertive language is needed to change inflationary expectations. President of the Chicago Fed Charles Evans has been leading the effort, arguing for an explicit policy statement that promises easy monetary policies until either inflation rises to 3% or unemployment drops to 7%. The Minneapolis Fed’s Narayana Kocherlakota believes similarly, but with thresholds of 2.25% inflation and 5.5% unemployment. And on November 13th, Vice Chairman Janet Yellen gave a speech at UC Berkeley’s Haas School of Business in support of her colleagues in Chicago and Minneapolis.

Now it’s time for Ben Bernanke and the rest of the Fed to get on the bandwagon. The minutes of the October 23-24 FOMC meeting suggest that the proposal for clear language on inflation is gaining traction, but for various technical and jargon-related reasons (they seem to be very concerned about the difference between a “trigger” and a “threshold”), no decision has been made.

It’s my hope that, come the next FOMC meeting on December 11th, the Fed will finally put its foot down and replace fuzzy language with clear and precise statements on inflation. The bleary-eyed undergrads will be thankful. So will the rest of us suffering from this pathologically awful economic slump.
