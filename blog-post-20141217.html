The Young, the Restless, and the Unemployed

  • Home
  • Blog
  • Blog post

November 21, 2014

"An unsold commodity is a nuisance, an unemployed worker a tragedy."
- Paul Krugman

Tragedy, unfortunately, is strikingly prevalent among young professionals in Taiwan. Simply put, the current level of youth unemployment there is absurd. An article recently published in CommonWealth Magazine points out that youth unemployment in Taiwan surpasses Japan and South Korea, and compares unfavorably with that of OECD countries. Both CommonWealth and my analysis suggest that the tragedy is structurally abnormal.



"Surpass Japan and catch up to Korea. Taiwan's youth unemployment rate exceeds 12%."
Source: CommonWealth Magazine


The Figures

To grasp the issue, I cobbled together employment data from the National Statistics website to create the charts below. The insights provided by the data are unsettling. While the unemployment rate across all demographic groups is currently around 4%, the rate among 20 to 24 year-olds is around 13% - the highest among all age groups. Unemployment in this age group has been consistently high since 2000, peaking at 15% in 2009.



To think about why 13% unemployment is not normal, consider that age is correlated with two counteracting factors that influence employability. The older you are, the more likely you are to have work experience. But the younger you are, the more likely you are to have skills that match the technological needs of a modern economy. 20 to 24 year-olds, on average, have less experience but more tech savvy. So while it is reasonable to expect them to have unemployment rates that are moderately higher than their older counterparts, their unemployment rates should not be exceptionally higher. 13% is 9 percentage points above the overall unemployment rate, which is simply absurd.

Next, look at the 25 to 29 year-olds. In theory, people within this age group should be the most employable - they acquired their degrees recently enough to have skills that match the current technological landscape, and are also old enough to have the requisite experience to succeed at work. However, the actual unemployment figures show that workers in this age group experience higher unemployment rates than all age groups other than 15 to 19 year-olds and 20 to 24 year-olds. This disparity between actual and expected unemployment trends suggests that Taiwan's labor market suffers from a structural problem.


The Theory

Unemployment figures based on educational attainment provide a clue to the source of the problem. Workers with university-level education and beyond display higher levels of unemployment than workers with lower levels of education. This suggests that labor supplied by Taiwan's higher education system does not match what the labor market actually demands.

This is the theory that CommonWealth Magazine offers as an explanation for Taiwan's unemployment problem. CommonWealth argues that, with the 1995 educational policy that upgraded all of Taiwan's technical and vocational schools to full-university status, the skills makeup of the labor supply dramatically changed, and became increasingly disconnected from what employers want from employees.

If this is indeed the case, it means that Taiwan's tragedy comes with an ironic twist. The whole point of higher education is the promise of gainful employment. For many of Taiwan's young college grads, this promise will turn out to be an empty one.



Overview

Taiwan's youth unemployment problem is structurally abnormal.
