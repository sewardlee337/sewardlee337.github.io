Of Gravity and Sunflowers

  • Home
  • Blog
  • Blog post

November 21, 2014

Many in Taiwan are averse to trading with mainland China. A dramatic example of this occurred earlier this year, in which activists of the Sunflower Student Movement occupied Taiwan’s legislature to protest a services trade pact with China. Their fears are understandable. The PRC does not hide the fact that it wants every interaction with Taiwan to be a step towards political unification.

But while the fears of the activists may be understandable, their policy views don't necessarily make economic sense. For example, a very common argument that they make is that Taiwan shouldn’t be trying to strengthen economic ties with China, but should instead focus on signing FTAs with other countries. As the Wall Street Journal reports, this view was reiterated by Democratic Progressive Party Secretary-General Joseph Wu last week, who stated: “It is clear [what] we need is not more China, but more of other countries.”



Actually, what's clear is that this view is driven by wishful thinking – not economics. Signing bilateral FTAs with other countries is important for its own sake, and it's something that Taiwan should keep on trying to do. But the resulting trade volume from FTAs with other countries would not nearly match the amount that Taiwan would enjoy by doing business with China.

Some simple back-of-the-envelope calculations involving the gravity model of trade can help illustrate this point. A version of the gravity model between Country i and Country j, as described in Krugman, Obstfeld, and Melitz (2012) textbook, is:

Tradeij = A × GDPi × GDPj / Distanceij

This means that, like celestial bodies in Newton's law of gravity, trade increases when the sizes of the two economies increase, and decreases when the distance between the two countries decreases. It's hard to escape the gravitational pull of a trading partner who's a large neighbor.

It should be obvious that you'd be hard-pressed to find a FTA signatory that would make a larger trade partner than China - China has the world's second largest economy, and is close enough for someone in Taiwan to swim to. But the data paint an even bleaker picture than that. Among the eight other countries that have signed FTAs with Taiwan, only Singapore is worth mentioning as a trade partner. And with 3.2% of China's GDP and a geographic proximity of 145% that of China, Taiwan-Singapore trade in a free trade regime is only 2.2% that of Taiwan-China trade in a free trade regime. Taiwan's trade with the Latin American signatories are not even worth mentioning when discussed in the the looming shadow of China.

Thus, currently none of Taiwan's other FTA partners can match China in terms of trade volume.

People like Mr. Wu will likely argue that, if Taiwan chooses not to engage in dealing with China, resources are freed to push pending FTA negotiations down the pipeline. The most hopeful prospect in matching China as a trade partner is the United States. The simple model that I implemented suggests that Taiwan-U.S. trade would be roughly half that of China. Is an FTA with the U.S. likely in the near future, and would the likelihood increase if Taiwan deemphasizes trade relations with China? Probably not - a FTA with the U.S. was proposed in 2000, and not much progress has been made over the past 14 years.

Now, a subtle point: I am not saying that Taiwan should free up all trade with China. That would be a political argument, not an economic one. And since Taiwan is a democracy, it's up to the Taiwanese people to decide which direction to take their economy via their political process. Furthermore, I believe that Taiwan's geopolitical qualms over dealing with China are 100% legitimate (regardless of whether I personally agree or disagree).

What I am saying is that China trade policy should be informed by an understanding of the relevant economic trade-offs. Proposals to simply look for more FTA partners as an alternative to trading with China suggest that the anti-trade camp does not comprehend the magnitude of the trade-offs.

So to the activists: You can keep your sunflowers, but please don't fool yourselves. China's your big fat neighbor, which makes it the trade partner that nobody else can match.
