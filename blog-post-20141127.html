The Twins Effect

  • Home
  • Blog
  • Blog post

November 27, 2014

In Taiwan, Korea is often portrayed as a close competitor - and sometimes as an archrival. This is because people in Taiwan generally understand that both countries have export-oriented economies, and that both produce a lot of the world's high tech electronics.

For example, in response to the recently-announced draft of the China-Korea FTA, an economist from the Taiwan Investment Security Advisory recently stated: "Taiwan should be very nervous about a China-South Korea FTA because of the high level of similarities between Taiwanese and Korean economies."



From an overly sensationalist political ad.
Source: NOWNews.com


But generalizations aside, exactly how do the two countries compare to each other in terms of international trade? To answer this question, I have compiled data provided by APEC and the World Trade Organization to create the visuals below. Tinker with the data, and you will quickly notice that:

  1. Korea exports more in absolute terms, but exports make up a greater share of Taiwan's economic activity.
  2. Both countries mainly export merchandise goods. Exports in commercial services are miniscule.
  3. Machinery makes up a significant share of merchandise exports for both countries, but there are notable differences in exactly what types of machinery they export.
  4. Taiwan and Korea had very similar volume and composition of trade in the 1990s and early 2000s. A noticeable divergence has occurred since then.

So there are important similarities, but they don't seem to be exact dopplegangers. And they were more similar in the past than they are today.

Now that we know this, the next level of investigation is to figure out why this is the case - whether the trends in the data reflect structural characteristics of the two economies, or political distortions (ie Taiwan's difficulties in signing FTAs due to lack of diplomatic recognition). This is likely something that I will try to address in a future post.
