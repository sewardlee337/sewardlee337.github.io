Lower Your Inflation Anxieties, Raise Those Inflation Targets

  • Home
  • Blog
  • Blog post

November 29, 2012

There’s a deeply-entrenched fear of inflation in our society. This is highly unfortunate, because I think it’s time for people (including some economists) to start viewing inflation as a legitimate policy tool. Given the dire economic straits that we are currently in, I’d consider modifying a sacrosanct piece of central banking policy. I propose raising the Federal Reserve’s inflation target to 4%.

Sacrosanct? Well, yes. Consider how, last week, the so-called “Shadow Open Market Committee” – a group that regularly critiques the Federal Open Market Committee – went into hysteria over what they perceived as Bernanke forsaking the 2% inflation target. Never mind whether their accusations were accurate. The large fuss made by these conservative economists is indicative of how deep the fear of inflation runs in both orthodoxy and the public consciousness. But no, the notion of raising the inflation target is not crazy. In fact, it’s supported by eminent economists, ranging from Nobel Laureate Paul Krugman, to IMF Chief Economist Olivier Blanchard, to UC Berkeley’s Brad Delong.

Some preliminary thoughts on inflation...

Our current level of inflation is at roughly 2% -- very, very close to what the Federal Reserve’s target rate is. Regardless of what the inflation fearmongers tell you, inflation and recessions usually don’t come hand-in-hand. Inflation happens when the increase in money liquidity causes an increase in demand, which in turn causes firms to raise their prices. So (barring freakish increases in prices of labor or components, of which there currently are none), if there’s no demand, there’s no inflation.

So, in our current situation, I’m not worried about inflation spiraling out of control. Neither should you be.


How does higher inflation help the economy?

The reason for raising the inflation target is that it raises the public’s expectations of future inflation. The U.S. economy is in a liquidity trap -- the nominal interest rate is zero and can’t go lower. The real interest rate is correspondingly stuck, and so the central bank can’t increase output and employment by lowering interest rates. Raising expected inflation lowers the real interest rate at which nominal rates are zero. This lifts us out of the liquidity trap, increases demand, and allows the Fed to slash interest rates once again.

In less wonkish and more human terms, say you are a businessperson undecided on whether to build a new factory. You hear on CNBC that inflation targets will go up, and inflation will probably go up as well. Suddenly, borrowing money to build that factory sounds like a good deal, since you expect that (thanks to inflation) the principal you need to pay back on a loan is worth less in real terms than the money you took out. Magnify that effect by the size of our economy, and suddenly we have a sizable increase in investment demand.

What’s more, increasing inflation targets decreases the chance of us falling into a liquidity trap in the future. As the San Francisco Fed’s John C. Williams pointed out in a 2009 paper, simulations show that a Fed following a calibrated Taylor rule with a 2% inflation target lands in a liquidity trap 10% of the time. A 4% target decreases the probability of us finding ourselves back in this type of mess.

The basic reasoning behind inflation targeting is the same as that of forward guidance (the topic of my previous post). Inflation targeting is, however, seen as a much more potent policy. Rather than cheap talk, a new inflation target is an explicit long-run policy commitment. Admittedly, it’s also more risky, because it puts the central bank’s credibility on the line.

Some might object to this policy on the grounds of it being too extreme. But 4% inflation really isn’t enough to debauch our economy’s price mechanism. In his book End This Depression Now!, Krugman pointed out that our economy was at 4% inflation during Reagan’s “Morning in America” era. Delong pointed out in The Economist that, in the 1990s, U.S. inflation fluctuated between 1.7% and 6%; in the mid-2000s, inflation fluctuated between 1.25% and 4%. To the self-proclaimed “Austrian economists” out there: I don’t recall the U.S. turning into Zimbabwe or Weimar Germany in any of these eras.

Bottom line: lower your inflation anxieties; raise those inflation targets. Raising the Federal Reserve’s target inflation is not that scary, and it might be just what we need.
