A Meditation on Positive Economics

  • Home
  • Blog
  • Blog post

December 1, 2012

I’ve received quite a bit of feedback regarding my previous posts on monetary policy. Some were insightful, some were provocative. After reading and processing the reaction, I’ve decided that it behooves me to lay out my views on what it means to really think like an economist.

On Economics as a Science

Despite the snide criticisms that the discipline has received in the wake of the recent financial and economic crises, there is no doubt in my mind that economics is a science. The philosophical foundation of economics is the same as that of your biology textbook and Bill Nye the Science Guy. They are all based on the Scientific Method – the notion that what we know about our world should come from rigorous examination and experimentation.

If economics is a science, then economic claims must adhere to the Scientific Method. The easiest way to test whether a claim is scientific is to see if the claim is falsifiable. You ask yourself: “Is there a way to prove that this claim is false?” This is because in science, a theory is only accepted if it survives repeated attempts to invalidate it. If you can’t subject a claim to such a test to begin with, the claim is not scientific..



Source:Becker Friedman Institute

For example, when one of my classmates with Marxist leanings (I’m from Berkeley, after all) claims that “capitalism will inevitably fail,” your first reaction should be to suspect that this is not a legitimate economic claim. Why? Because by invoking inevitability, this claim implicitly suggests that no matter what any test or experiment suggests, the claim cannot be falsified. Again, if you can’t test whether a claim is false, the claim is not scientific.

On Economics and Personal Values

As human beings, we all have our own personal values. This is good and all, but it creates a conundrum: what if our personal values influence our economic analysis? I believe the answer comes from the work of John Neville Keynes (father of Maynard Keynes), which was subsequently documented in Milton Friedman’s Essays in Positive Economics.

Neville Keynes argued for preserving the objectivity of economics by creating a clear separation between “positive economics” and “normative economics.” “Positive economics” is an analysis of what the data says and what the theories suggest. “Normative economics” is an opinion based upon personal values. The former must always come before the latter – we should apply our values after we see what the data says. We must strive to never let our values bias our interpretation of the data, and never invoke values without looking at the data at all.

So, if you hear someone – let’s say one of the three Pauls in Washington (Ron, Rand, and Paul Ryan) – claim that a program is bad because it “leads to big government threatening our economic liberty,” an immediate sense of skepticism should arise within you. This claim invokes “liberty” – a values-based word that makes us feel warm and fuzzy – but there is not an iota of economic reasoning behind it.

On the other hand, it’s okay if an antitrust litigator says that the monopolization of an industry is bad because “it restricts the choices of consumers while forcing them to pay higher prices.” This is because the conclusion here is firmly based on analysis, not simply moralistic posturing.

On Schools of Economics

In the news, we often hear about different schools of economics: the New Keynesians, the Chicago School, the Austrian School, etc. Often, they’re paired up with one another, like liberal versus conservative economists, or freshwater versus saltwater economists. This makes it very tempting to divide economists into categories, as we do for politicians and constituencies.

This is highly unfortunate, because economists should not regurgitate a school’s tenet, as many politicians do their party platform. They need to produce careful analysis on a case-by-case basis. This is why I tell people that, while I’ve been educated by economists popularly deemed “New Keynesians,” I am first and foremost an empiricist.

On Being Wrong

At one point or another, I will be wrong. This happens to all economic bloggers and all economists. If there is one iron-clad law to the universe, it is that humans are fallible. What matters, then, is how to deal with being wrong.

I think the most lucid line ever spoken by an economist is John Maynard Keynes’s famous quip: “When the facts change, I change my mind. What do you do, sir?” This is an attitude that every economics thinker needs to possess. And no, it’s not the same as Mitt Romney changing his views on abortion for the sake of political expediency. The former is a matter of having the courage to admit that your previous analyses are not applicable in the present circumstances. The latter is simply twisting your values to pander to an electorate.
