Book Review: Antifragile

  • Home
  • Blog
  • Blog post

December 29, 2012

It disappoints me to have to write a mixed review for Nassim Nicholas Taleb's Antifragile. While I disagreed with much of his previous two books, Fooled by Randomness (2001) and The Black Swan (2005), I found them to be clever and immensely enjoyable. I cannot say the same for Taleb's latest work. Antifragile had promise, but its ascent to greatness is derailed by Taleb's ambition to delegitimize economics and social science.

Nassim Taleb's Antifragile is intended to be the third act of his Incerto trilogy – a series of statistical and epistemological essays on uncertainty and volatility. It takes off where the previous books left off. In Fooled By Randomness, Taleb explained that we have a tendency to underestimate the amount of volatility in the world. In his eponymously-named sequel, Taleb introduced the concept of the Black Swan: low-probability, high-impact events that are fundamentally unpredictable. Antifragile is supposed to conclude the discussion by explaining how to deal with uncertainty and Black Swans in disciplines ranging from business to law, politics to finance.



The Lernaean Hydra – an example of antifragility from antiquity.


The first third of the book is sensible enough. Taleb categorizes everything into three categories: fragile, robust, and antifragile. Fragile things -- such as pre-2008 Wall Street or your mother's fine china plates – are weakened by random stressors. In contrast, antifragile things -- such as Silicon Valley venture capital or your body's muscular system -- thrive under volatility. Things that are robust are neutral to volatility.

From this triad, Taleb manages to derive a set of decision-making heuristics that can be employed by policymakers in many different domains. For example, Taleb argues that designing antifragile systems involves focusing on reaction rather than prediction. This is advice that defense and homeland security experts should take into account, given the tendency of terrorist attacks and geopolitical crises to evade prediction. Taleb also explains that efficiency at the expense of antifragility can lead to traumatic consequences in the long run. This is as true for an overly-leveraged Wall Street bank as it is for a Lamborghini speeding through downtown Manhattan at 120 mph.



A modern attempt to invoke antifragility.


However, from roughly Chapter 13: "Lecturing Birds on How to Fly", Taleb starts getting very sloppy. He begins by attempting to argue that all economic and social science theories suffer from spurious relationship problems (what he refers to as "epiphenomena"). Taleb's exposition of this problem, however, displays a remarkably elementary understanding of the search for cause-and-effect in science. He argues that the development of theories in social science is analogous to "lecturing birds on flying and believing the lecture is the cause of those wonderful skills". This is a gross mischaracterization of social science research, which in fact involves rigorous observation and experimentation – via the laboratory, natural experiments, or regression analysis. Furthermore, he categorically labels all economists as "Soviet-Harvard" intellectuals, and proceeds to generally mischaracterize their research. I would say that much of his critiques against "Soviet-Harvard" thinkers are attacks against straw men, but he does so with a sense of zeal reserved for assailing windmills.

It gets worse. Taleb goes on to argue that due to their tendency to produce prediction errors, all scientific theories are useless. To be antifragile, he claims, means that you neither need to know nor predict anything at all. Taleb fails to understand that knowledge and theory derived from science is primarily for explanation; prediction is secondary in importance. Without science, you simply cannot satisfy a basic human need to know.

This is not to mention all the economic arguments throughout Antifragile that Taleb fails to back up with more than anecdotal evidence and philosophical musing. For example, his claim that the effects of Federal Reserve open market operations is unproven is terminally silly (read Milton Friedman and Anna Schwartz's A Monetary History of the United States). Furthermore, he makes the nonsensical argument that imperial China failed to spark an indigenous Industrial Revolution because it lacked stochastic tinkering – it didn't have enough Steve Jobses. The World Bank's Justin Yifu Lin has shown that trial-and-error tinkering wasn't missing in imperial China, but innnovation via the Scientific Method was. (Other theories include the fact that imperial China had did not have a robust civil society, or that China's labor surplus disincentivized the invention of labor-saving technologies. All of these proposals make more sense than Taleb's Asian Steve Jobs argument.)

I find the crux of Antifragile's problems to be two-fold. First, Nassim Taleb's individual concepts and heuristics are robust; the theories he derives from them are as fragile as they come. While he denigrates the Scientific Method, his own epistemological theories and economic claims largely fail under close scrutiny. Second, the implicit message behind his theories is problematic. In his previous works, he essentially argued: "You know nothing; display humility when facing the limits of your knowledge." In Antifragile, the message is: "You need to know nothing; arrogantly flaunt your ignorance."

At one point, Taleb invokes a concept called Bergson's razor: "A philosopher should be known for one single idea, not more". While I like Taleb's general idea of antifragility, I am struggling to forget many of his terrible arguments regarding science and causality. For his own sake, he should hope that I succeed.
