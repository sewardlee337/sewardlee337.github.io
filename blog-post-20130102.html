Book Review: The Signal and the Noise

  • Home
  • Blog
  • Blog post

January 2, 2013

Nate Silver is a phenomenon. After displaying the uncanny ability to predict the results of the 2008, 2010, and 2012 elections, the mind behind the blog FiveThirtyEight has reached a celebrity status that few statisticians dare dream of reaching.

The Signal and the Noise is the most sophisticated book that I've read in a long time, and it is proof that Silver's celebrity is well-deserved. Drawing from his experience as a statistician and political pundit, Silver endeavors to instruct readers on the science and art of prediction. What he ends up producing is a Freakonomics-style adventure in applied statistics, which also happens to be a Bayesian inference textbook in disguise.



Silver at a book signing
Source: Wikimedia Commons (originally Jack Newton, Flickr)


If we generate 2.5 quintillion bytes of data each day, why are the predictive models we use – in almost every scientific endeavor – so abysmally inaccurate? It's because we are bad at parsing through and interpreting the data. As Silver puts it, "The signal is truth. The noise is what distracts us from the truth." The relationships that we seek in an ocean of data are likely to be obscured by random and irrelevant patterns. Silver shows that the problem of separating signal from noise is ubiquitous in all disciplines that involve statistical prediction. On this note, Silver takes his readers on a fascinating romp through a vast array of scientific disciplines, including financial engineering, plate tectonics, electoral politics, meteorology, and epidemiology.

Half of the book consists of a tour through the vast graveyard of failed predictive models. Along the way, Silver points out why each model failed. Some fail because of methodological errors. For example, the earthquake model used in the construction of the Fukushima nuclear reactor was overfit – it missed the true statistical relationship because it was built to chase irrelevant data points in a small data sample. Such a model underestimated the likelihood of the 2011 Tōhoku earthquake striking the region. In the case of the 2008 financial meltdown, rating agencies like Moody's and Standard & Poor were forecasting out of the sample – they used old models built on historical data to analyze a completely novel scenario (the probability of mortgage-backed securities blowing up).


Aftemath of the 2011 Tōhoku earthquake


Silver notes that predictive failure can also stem from hubris. Describing the difference between FiveThirtyEight and political pundits on The McLaughlin Group, he categorizes forecasters into hedgehogs and foxes, after a classic study written by psychologist Philip Tetlock. Hedgehogs believe in a world governed by simple relationships and cherry-pick new information to confirm pre-existing biases. Foxes see the world in complex probabilistic terms and modify their predictions in light of new information. Any predictive endeavor that employs hedgehogs is a catastrophe waiting to happen -- just look at how the pollsters on Mitt Romney's political campaign performed.

The solution to our prediction problems, Silver asserts, comes in the form of Bayesian statistics, often neglected in standard classroom curricula in favor of R.A. Fisher's frequentist statistics. Bayesian inference forces you to repeatedly modify your prediction in the presence of new information. Every modification of your predictive model takes you on an asymptotic path, closer to the true statistical relationship you are trying to understand. Silver illustrates this concept by invoking John Maynard Keynes's immortal quip: "When the facts change, I change my mind. What do you do, sir?"

While The Signal and the Noise touches upon Bayesian statistics, chaos theory, complexity theory, and game theory, prior knowledge of these fields is not necessary to appreciate this book. It would help, however, if you are well-versed in baseball, chess, and poker. Silver uses many examples from these games to illustrate his points and dedicates three entire chapters to them. (At the very least, I suggest that you watch Brad Pitt's Moneyball.) There are, obviously, some graphs and equations in this book, but trudging through the math will pay enormous dividends.

If you are even remotely interested in politics and policy-making, you owe it to yourself to read The Signal and the Noise. Very few books on statistical prediction are as meticulously executed and engrossing as this. Even fewer will be as helpful at preparing you to critically analyze political and economic policy. I predict that Nate Silver will dazzle your mind and teach you to see the world in a significantly more nuanced way.
