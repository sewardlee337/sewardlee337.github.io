The Lesser Depression

  • Home
  • Blog
  • Blog post

December 19, 2012

Technically, what history books call the "Great Depression" wasn't one long recession. It consisted of two recessions (1929-1933 and 1937-1938) and a decade-long uphill climb back to natural levels of output and employment. So it's understandable why, given how we're still slowly crawling out of the last recession, some now call what we face the "Lesser Depression."

Such were my thoughts when I saw the graph below After the Federal Reserve announced that it was aiming for a 6.5% unemployment rate, the Hamilton Project's Michael Looney and Adam Greenberg decided to forecast how long getting there would take. What they ended up producing is a graph full of depressing news. The bottom line is that, assuming that we add 220,000 jobs per month (the average growth rate over the past year), we won't get to a 6.5% unemployment rate until mid-2015. We won't hit the Fed's unemployment target for three years.



Length of time it will take to reach 6.5% unemployment, assuming different rates of job growth
Source: The Hamilton Project

There's additional bad news to consider - Dylan Matthews pointed out on Washington Post's Wonkblog that while 6.5% is an improvement over our current situation, it's not close to the level we had prior to the recession. Furthermore, our official unemployment statistics don't take into account discouraged workers (workers so chronically unemployed that they're not counted in the labor force) or underemployed workers (workers who work part-time, but need to work full-time). A 6.5% unemployment rate doesn't take into account many people who would still be suffering.


The fact that we're arguably in a "Lesser Recession" has been resonating in our political rhetoric for a long time now. No, what we are facing is not normal, particularly if you associate "normal" with recessions we've faced between World War II and the mid-2000s. That said, Mitt Romney's claim that our "unnatural" recovery is somehow President Obama's fault was grossly unfair. To understand why, there are three points to consider:

  1. Most previous post-WWII recessions were created by the Federal Reserve to fight inflation. Since the Fed caused these recessions, it also had control over the subsequent recoveries. This is not the case with our latest recession.
  2. We just had a financial crisis, and financial crises lead to deeper recessions and longer recoveries. This is a point that economists Carmen Reinhardt and Kenneth Rogoff painstakingly showed in their (ironically-named) book, This Time It's Different.
  3. We are currently in a liquidity trap. Our federal funds rate is effectively zero. The Fed can no longer promote recovery by lowering short-term interest rates.

None of these are the Obama Administration's fault. Also, these three points are characteristic of both our current situation and the mess we had in the 1930s.

With things the way they are, be thankful that the Fed has shown renewed signs of taking the employment side of its dual mandate seriously. Recall that, last week, the Fed decided to increase open market purchases of mortgage-backed securities and issued numerical thresholds for unemployment and inflation. Considering that unemployment is high and inflation is negligible, these are both sensible policies.

One more thing: remember how the Great Depression consisted of two recessions, and we've only faced one so far? Well... we still have the fiscal cliff to worry about, which may well trigger another recession if not handled properly. Here's hoping that the politicking in Washington will lead to a sensible deal on the fiscal cliff.
