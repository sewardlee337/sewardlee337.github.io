Moving Ahead with Forward Guidance

  • Home
  • Blog
  • Blog post

December 14, 2012

It’s no secret that I’m a vocal advocate of having the Federal Reserve change its communications policy. Therefore, it should be no surprise that I’m rather pleased by the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) statement issued on December 12th, which announced that the Fed is adopting numerical thresholds for unemployment and inflation.

Specifically, the FOMC decided that a federal funds rate between 0% and 0.25% is appropriate if unemployment remains above 6.5% and inflation is forecast to not exceed 2.5%. Granted, the adoption of numerical thresholds is a small step for the Fed, and not at all a giant leap for our economy. That being said, I believe that it’s a step in the right direction.

The Fed’s new numerical thresholds are a marked improvement over its previous communications policy. Announcements by the Federal Reserve – derisively known as “Fedspeak” – have traditionally been shrouded in opaque language that leaves the public scratching its heads. Yet despite how often Alan Greenspan vouches for the importance of incoherently mumbled announcements, the research has shown that clear language can play an important role in monetary policy. Clear and assertive language helps the public understand what to expect from the Fed, which in turn helps them form expectations regarding the future economy. The use of signaling and communication to change future expectations is known in policy circles as “forward guidance”.



The Federal Open Market Committee


Specifically, I’m hoping that the Fed’s new forward guidance policy will lead to an increase in the public’s expectation of future inflation. Recall that we are in a liquidity trap – nominal interest rates are at zero, and the Fed can no longer stimulate the economy by lowering the federal funds rate. If the Fed can convince people that future inflation will rise, people will also expect that the principle they pay on a loan will be worth less in real terms than the money they borrow from the bank. This creates an incentive to invest, thereby stimulating the economy to produce more output.

The beauty of numerical thresholds is that they now give people a credible reason to believe in higher future inflation. The new statement tells us that the Fed is okay with projected inflation of up to 2.5% -- higher than the 2% long-run target where the public’s expectations were previously anchored.

There’s some jargon-related technicalities that are worth bringing up here. What the Fed is adopting are numerical "thresholds," not "triggers." If a macroeconomic variable were to reach a "trigger," the Fed would automatically adjust its policy to bring the variable back to acceptable levels. Whereas if a variable reaches a "threshold," the Fed only promises to deliberate and put policy change into consideration. So if our inflation rate is projected to exceed 2.5%, the Fed has only promised to hold a meeting and discuss what to do. Note that the Fed’s long-term inflation “target” doesn’t change. The Fed has made it very very clear that the target is still at 2%.

Despite my positive outlook over this policy change, you may wonder how much of a substantive difference it will make for our sluggish economy. There are potential flaws to the new strategy. Most notably, the University of Oregon’s Mark Thoma points out that the new forward guidance policy won’t do much if the public’s inflationary expectations continue to be anchored at 2%. In addition, Paul Krugman is characteristically gloomy, arguing that the Fed’s decision amounts to not much more than a promise by Chairman Bernanke not to do anything stupid.

Even so, the new communications policy should be considered welcome news. It’s a sign that, despite the powerful hold that orthodoxy has on central banking, Bernanke’s Fed is showing a willingness to experiment and innovate its way to recovery. Our economy is still weak and we are far from firm footing, but at least we are heading in the right direction.
